## What is a Bible Commentary?

At its core, a Bible commentary is a written systematic explanation and interpretation of Scripture. Think of it as a guided tour through the biblical text. Scholars and theologians spend years studying the original languages (Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek), the historical settings, and the literary structures of the Bible to help bridge the gap between the ancient world and our modern lives.

The primary purpose of a commentary is clarity. It helps answer the "Who, what, where, and why" of a passage, providing context that might not be immediately obvious to a 21st-century reader.

## How to Use Commentaries in Your Study

While reading the Bible, you may encounter difficult phrases, cultural customs that seem strange, or theological concepts that are hard to grasp. This is where a commentary shines. You can use them to:

- Unpack Cultural Context: Understand why a specific gesture or law was significant to the original audience.
- Clarify Language: Learn the nuances of the original Greek or Hebrew words used by the authors.
- Connect the Dots: See how a specific verse fits into the broader narrative of the entire Bible.

## A Diverse History of Insight

Commentaries vary greatly depending on when and by whom they were written. On this site, you will find a wide range of perspectives:

- Classic & Patristic Works: Some commentaries are hundreds of years old, offering a window into how the early Church or the Reformers understood the Word.
- Modern Scholarship: Recent commentaries often benefit from new archaeological discoveries and advanced linguistic tools.

Because these works span centuries, the language and cultural biases of the authors will vary. Reading an older commentary can be like stepping back in time; it requires a bit of "cultural translation" but offers timeless spiritual depth.

## A Note on Discernment and Authority

It is important to remember that while we provide these commentaries as a resource, inclusion on this site does not constitute a blanket endorsement of every claim or theological position held by the authors.

Commentaries are written by fallible humans. While they are incredibly helpful tools, they are not inspired in the same way the Bible is. 

> Commentaries are the *aid*, but Scripture is the *authority*.

### Keeping the Main Thing, the Main Thing
Your study should always follow this priority:
- Scripture First: Read the text for yourself before turning to a commentator.
- Prayerful Reflection: Ask for the Holy Spirit’s guidance as you read.
- Consulting the Commentary: Use the commentary to test your findings or clear up confusion.

Never let a scholar’s opinion replace your own prayerful meditation on the Word. Use these resources to sharpen your understanding, but always return to the Source.
